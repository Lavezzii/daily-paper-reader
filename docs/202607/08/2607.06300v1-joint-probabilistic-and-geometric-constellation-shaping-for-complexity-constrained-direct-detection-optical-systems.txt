# Joint Probabilistic and Geometric Constellation Shaping for Complexity-Constrained Direct Detection Optical Systems 

Rodrigo Fischer (1) , Shrinivas Chimmalgi (1) , Andrej Rode (1) , Laurent Schmalen (1) (1) Communications Engineering Lab (CEL), Karlsruhe Institute of Technology (KIT) * Corresponding author: rodrigo.fischer@kit.edu 

Abstract We evaluate joint probabilistic and geometric constellation shaping via reinforcement learning for complexity-constrained joint equalization and demodulation of direct detection optical signals. We demonstrate the proposed technique in a simulated 56 GBd, 2.2 km C-band direct-detection system, demonstrating its effectiveness for complexity-constrained receivers. ©2026 The Author(s) 

Introduction 

While direct detection (DD) remains the preferred technology for short-reach optical links due to its advantages in cost and power consumption [1], it can introduce significant impairments to the sig-nal. Specifically, DD combined with the effect of chromatic dispersion ( CD ) accumulated over the fiber results in an equivalent non-linear channel with memory. To mitigate this, various strategies have been explored; notably, machine learning-based non-linear digital signal processing ( DSP )has been evaluated for low-complexity equaliza-tion in DD links [2–4]. At the same time, constellation shaping ( CS )is required to close the gap to channel capac-ity [5] and has already been assessed for DD systems [6, 7]. However, constraining the com-putational complexity of the DSP at the receiver impacts the achievable information rate in the form of an equivalent degraded channel, and the analy-sis of the effect of such constraints on the shaped constellations remains an open challenge. In this work, we use soft thresholding algo-rithms [8] in order to constrain the computational complexity of convolutional neural network ( CNN )-based joint equalizers and demodulators, com-bined with reinforcement learning ( RL ) [9] tech-niques in order to perform joint probabilistic and geometric constellation shaping via the general-ized mutual information ( GMI ) [10], a metric that is able to take the degradation introduced by the constrained receiver into account during shaping.

Training the Detector 

We optimize θ via proximal gradient descent [8], where a L1 regularization penalty is introduced in order to induce sparsity in θ. Setting fewer pa-rameters to nonzero reduces the complexity of the detector in terms of real valued multiplications per-formed, and also in terms of the memory required to store the parameters.

Simulation Results 

We simulated a 2.2 km long fiber with a disper-sion coefficient of 16.3 ps · nm −1 · km −1 and a carrier wavelength of 1540 nm. We considered 2,4,8PAM signals modulated at 56 GBd with sym-bols C ⊂ R; bipolar signals can be generated with a single Mach-Zehnder modulator for the in-phase component. We used a batch size of 64 sequences and a batch length of 512 symbols. We perform filtering in the frequency domain, meaning all convolutions are cyclic. In order to simulate the full bandwidth of the signals, we used a simulation oversampling factor of Nos,sim = 4 . We limited the number of gradient steps to 8000, with a grace period of 1000 steps where C, P are not updated, only the detector. We found this to be vital for convergence, since we want to avoid C, P to adapt to a poorly fitted detector. We use the GMI as a performance metric and the number of real-valued multiplications per bit ( rvmb ) as a computational complexity metric. In order to explore a large range of complexities, we trained an ensemble of CNN architectures with 1 to 4 layers with varying kernel sizes for each layer and stored the achieved GMI , generating a 2D set suitable for multi-objective optimization, from which we extract the Pareto fronts. In Fig. 2 we optimize C, P, L i(·, θ) for the case where either the optical or electrical noise domi-nates. We find that under our simulation settings, where the detector complexity is constrained, the performance is dictated only by the noise level and doesn’t change if νel = 0 or νopt = 0 . Conclusions 

Through extensive simulation results, we demon-strated that high achievable rates with DD are still possible with low-complexity non-linear DSP and constellation shaping. Furthermore, our find-ings indicate that while joint optimization is critical for 8PAM, geometric shaping alone offers a low-complexity alternative for 4PAM, since we avoid the implementation challenges of DM s. These in-sights provide a clear framework for optimizing DD systems considering the specific cost and power constraints of next-generation optical systems. Acknowledgements
