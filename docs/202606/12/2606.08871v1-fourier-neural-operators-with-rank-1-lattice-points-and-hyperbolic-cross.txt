# Fourier Neural Operators with Rank-1 Lattice Points and Hyperbolic Cross 

Jakob Dilen, Alexander Keller, Frances Y. Kuo, Dirk Nuyens June 2026 

Abstract 

The Fourier neural operator (FNO) is a neural network architecture that learns mappings between function spaces. Its efficient implementation is based on the multi-dimensional Fourier transform. By deriving general regularity bounds for the FNO with respect to both the spatial and parametric variables, we prove that the generalization error of the FNO can be improved by replacing spatial tensor product grids with purpose-built rank-1 lattice points, and by using a second lattice carefully constructed as training points in the parametric space. We achieve more accurate and efficient approximations from fewer network parameters, fewer spatial points, and fewer training samples. In addition, the architecture is simplified, because the high-dimensional Fourier transform on rank-1 lattices requires only a one-dimensional fast Fourier transform , and we can use a hyperbolic cross frequency index set with lattice points. We demonstrate the benefits of our lattice-based hyperbolic-cross FNOs for an elliptic PDE on the torus. 

# 1 Introduction 

We consider the problem of solving the Data-to-Observables map with operators between two infinite-dimensional spaces: Given data a ∈ A, compute observable G(a) ∈ Z, (1) where A and Z are separable Banach spaces of functions on a bounded physical domain D ⊂ Rd

taking values in Rda and Rdu , respectively. As an example, consider the elliptic partial differential equation (PDE) 

−∇ · (a∇u) = f in D, (2) possibly together with some boundary condition on ∂D . Here a is the input scalar permeability field, f is the forcing term, and the PDE solution u is the scalar pressure field. The observable may be G(a) = u so that da = du = 1, and we may take Z = H1(D) to be the standard first-order Sobolev space on the physical domain D. As another observable, G(a) = ∇u might be the velocity field, in which case da = 1 and du = d.The aim is to learn an approximation Gθ : A → Z to the operator G with parameters θ from some finite-dimensional parameter space Θ. Following Li et al. (2021) and Kovachki et al. (2023), we consider a Fourier neural operator (FNO ) as our approximation Gθ. The new contribution of this paper is that we deploy quasi-Monte Carlo (Niederreiter, 1992; Dick et al., 2013) methods on two fronts: we use a rank-1 lattice (Sloan and Joe, 1994; Dick et al., 2022) with respect to the physical variable x to generate efficient data points in the physical domain, and we use a second, different rank-1 lattice with respect to the parametric variable y to generate effective training points in the parametric domain (Keller et al., 2026a,b). We also make use of a hyperbolic cross 

in the frequency domain to accompany our lattice points in the physical domain (Cools et al., 2010, 2020, 2021; Kuo et al., 2025). In more detail, our three contributions are: 1

1. Observations are taken at a discrete set of data points on the physical domain D. This is typically taken to be a regular grid on D and can be computationally expensive for a fine grid in dimension d = 3 or higher. We replace a regular grid by a specifically selected rank-1 lattice in the physical domain D. This drastically decreases the number of required spatial points while maintaining similar accuracy. 2. The input field a(x, y) for x ∈ D is typically generated by a series expansion in terms of random parameters y from some parametric space Y , e.g., an affine random field model or a Chebyshev model (see Section 3.1 ahead). In this case, we can view a(x, y) as a hard-coded initial layer of the neural operator. We replace random parametric sampling by a carefully constructed rank-1 lattice as training points in the parametric domain Y , to improve the convergence of the generalization error. 3. We assume that we are approximating functions with a certain prescribed smoothness, characterized by the decay of their Fourier coefficients. This justifies the truncation of the frequency domain Zd to a finite index set A. Typically this is taken to be an axis-parallel box, which bounds all components in an index h ∈ A by some prescribed magnitude. For suitable classes of functions, replacing such a box by a hyperbolic cross in the frequency domain Zd allows us to use much fewer indices while maintaining similar accuracy. Solving PDEs using model order reduction techniques to approximate the underlying oper-ator has been the main focus of a lot of research (Shahane et al., 2019; Bhattacharya et al., 2021; Khoo et al., 2021; Pestourie et al., 2023; Novak et al., 2024). One of its main branches of research has been the use of neural networks (Raissi et al., 2019; Bhattacharya et al., 2021; Khoo et al., 2021; Pestourie et al., 2023). More recently some of this focus has shifted to address the fact that these operators act on function spaces instead of vectors or tensors, resulting in the development of the neural operator (Li et al., 2020a,b, 2021; Lu et al., 2021; Jin et al., 2022; Kovachki et al., 2023; Lanthaler, 2023). Some promising examples are the PCA-net (Lanthaler, 2023) that makes use of the principal component analysis, and the DeepONet (Lu et al., 2021; Jin et al., 2022) that uses a projecting-lifting structure by combining two models. The architecture of choice in this paper is the Fourier neural operator or FNO (Li et al., 2021; Kovachki et al., 2023), which uses the properties of convolutions in the Fourier frequency space to apply integral kernel operators in an efficient manner. Aside from its empirical performance, the theoretical aspects of the FNO have recently also been studied, including the universal approximation theorem (Kovachki et al., 2021) for the FNO, as well as the role that non-locality plays herein (Lanthaler et al., 2025a). While the universal approximation theorem proves that a neural network/operator can represent a function, expression rates (Schwab and Zech, 2019, 2023; Herrmann et al., 2024) measure the efficiency of a network architecture and specify how big the network needs to be. Other extensions and alternatives to the FNO include: the adaptive Fourier neural operator or AFNO (Guibas et al., 2022) that was initially developed for use in image analysis; the Laplace neural operator or LNO (Cao et al., 2024) which is an alternative to the FNO that specializes in handling transient behavior; and the spherical Fourier neural operator or SFNO (Bonev et al., 2023, 2025) for learning operators on spherical geometries, well-suited for forecasting atmospheric dynamics. The practical performance of FNO is still a long way from the theory (Lanthaler et al., 2025b; Liu et al., 2024), just as observed for the neural network (Cybenko, 1989; Hornik et al., 1989; Hornik, 1991; Voigtlaender, 2023). For related research on how to address this problem for neural networks, we refer to the review of Adcock et al. (2024). Addressing this problem for the FNO is part of the focus of the current paper. 

Quasi-Monte Carlo points have been deployed as training points instead of random samples for deep neural networks (Lye et al., 2020; Mishra and Rusch, 2021; Longo et al., 2021; Keller 2et al., 2026a,b). They can reduce the generalization error (for unseen data) via a proven, better convergence rate for the generalization gap , which is the difference between the generalization error and the training error observed in practice. The convergence theory requires knowledge of the parametric regularity of the neural network, i.e., the mixed derivatives of the neural network with respect to the parametric variables y. Hence, Keller et al. (2026a) derived explicit parametric regularity bounds for general deep neural networks. The bounds depend on the network parameters θ and the choice of a smooth activation function, with explicit constants. These bounds are fully general and remain independent of both the target function and the training data. Keller et al. (2026a) also devised a tailored regularization strategy during training to encourage the network parameters to match the regularity bounds of a given target function. Keller et al. (2026b) further extended the theory to a generalized sigmoid activation function which in the limit becomes ReLU. The philosophy of Keller et al. (2026a,b) is very similar to the 

derivative informed neural operator or DINO (O’Leary-Roseberry et al., 2024) where training is done with gradient matching in mind. Following the framework in Keller et al. (2026a,b), in this paper we prove general regularity bounds for the FNO, with respect to not only the parametric variables y but now also the physical variables x. Instead of a regularization strategy, the regularity information of the target function is hard-coded as an initial layer of our FNO. These regularity bounds together with known theory of lattice rules for integration (Dick et al., 2013, 2022) allow us to conclude a theoretical convergence rate for the generalization error , see our main result–Theorem 5 below. The task of establishing the universal approximation theorem for our “ lattice-based hyperbolic-cross FNO ” is the focus of a subsequent paper Dilen et al. (2026), which will build on known theory of lattice-based algorithms for function approximation (Cools et al., 2020, 2021; Kuo et al., 2025). Lattice rules and other quasi-Monte Carlo methods have been successfully applied, with full theoretical justification, to problems in uncertainty quantification involving parametric PDEs. Some recent examples are wave propagation and scattering (Graham et al., 2026), tumor growth and treatment (Gilbert et al., 2026), PDE-constrained optimal control (Guth et al., 2024), PDEs with random domains (Hakula et al., 2024), probability density estimation (Gilbert et al., 2025), kernel interpolation (Kaarnioja et al., 2022); see Kuo and Nuyens (2016) for a survey of earlier theoretical works. In all cases we have fast construction (Nuyens and Cools, 2006) of the points to achieve good convergence rates with errors bounded independently of the parametric dimension. In the case of the FNO, lattice points are particularly beneficial for the physical space because they can replace computing the d-dimensional FFT on a regular grid by a 1-dimensional FFT 

(Keiner et al., 2010; K¨ ammerer et al., 2015, 2021; Kuo et al., 2021). Further more, the frequency index set can be reduced to a hyperbolic cross under favorable conditions (Cools et al., 2020, 2021; Kuo et al., 2025). The structure of this paper is as follows. In Section 2 we define the Fourier neural operator (FNO), explain how to apply the hyperbolic cross in the frequency domain, and introduce the application of the Fourier transform on rank-1 lattices within the FNO. In Section 3 we discuss the generalization and training errors for our FNO, and specify our random field models for the scalar field a(x, y) and the theoretical settings for error analysis. We then analyze the regularity with respect to the parametric variables y in Section 3.3 and with respect to the spatial variables x in Section 3.4, ending with our combined generalization error bound in our main result–Theorem 5. We present our numerical results in Section 4 and finally draw the conclusion in Section 5. The technical proofs of our theorems are given in Appendix A. 32 Fourier Neural Operator (FNO)

In machine learning, each of the output dimensions {d1, . . . , d L+1 } is called a channel . The number of these intermediate output dimensions may vary, as dℓ = dℓ+1 does not have to hold. 4.3 Training Setup 

The models are trained with the mini-batch Adam optimization algorithm (Kingma and Ba, 2015) with batch size 16, using an adaptive “reduce learning rate on plateau” scheduler with reduction factor 0 .8, patience 50, and an initial learning rate of 10 −2. For the experiments in Section 4.4 and Section 4.5, the networks are trained for 2000 epochs on a NVIDIA GeForce P100 GPU, while for the timing study in Section 4.6 training is carried out on the CPU to better expose the influence of the parameter count on the training time. All architectures employ linear lifting and projecting operators P and Q and use L = 2 hidden layers with dℓ = 16 channels each. We have a scalar input (affine model) field and consider the quantity of interest G(x, y) = u(x, y), so da = du = 1. However, as is standard, we use positional embedding, resulting in da = 3. The standard FNO is implemented using the NeuralOperator package (Kossaifi et al., 2026), and the code for the lattice-based variants is made available on GitHub. # 5 Conclusion 

The paper shows that Fourier neural operators (FNOs) can be combined with rank-1 lattices in both the spatial and parametric domains and with hyperbolic-cross frequency truncation to obtain provable theoretical generalization error bounds. For a benchmark elliptic PDE, we demonstrate lower parameter counts and training cost, while maintaining or slightly improving predictive accuracy. The rigorous theoretical foundation provides avenues of future research, especially the practical exploration of higher-dimensional domains and the application of rank-1 lattice based FNOs to other practical problems. The universal approximation theory for our lattice-based hyperbolic cross FNOs is the focus of a subsequent paper (Dilen et al., 2026). 24 Acknowledgments
