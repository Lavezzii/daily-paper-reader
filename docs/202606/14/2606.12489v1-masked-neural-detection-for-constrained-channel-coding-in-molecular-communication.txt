# Masked Neural Detection for Constrained Channel Coding in Molecular Communication 

Melih ¸ Sahin , Graduate Student Member, IEEE, Ozgur B. Akan , Fellow, IEEE 

Abstract —Molecular communication (MC) suffers from severe diffusion memory because molecules released for one symbol may arrive during later symbols. Neural sequence detectors, especially sliding bidirectional recurrent neural networks (SBRNNs), can substantially outperform threshold detectors in such channels. This raises a central question for MC channel coding: does a code whose advantage was established under threshold detection retain it when both coded and uncoded transmission are evaluated with neural detection? This letter answers this question for run-length-limited ISI-mitigation (RLIM) codes, a class of constrained codes previously shown to provide large BER gains in MC. Across the tested operating points, the best RLIM-SBRNN receiver beats the best uncoded receiver, chosen between threshold and SBRNN detection, in 46 of 59 cases, with a mean gain of 10 .36 × over those wins. We also propose an RLIM-tailored training mask for compact SBRNN detectors, improving the unmasked RLIM-SBRNN in 227 of 236 comparisons with 3.267 × mean gain when masking is beneficial. Finally, the compact masked RLIM-SBRNN is competitive with channel-state-aware MLSE despite using no channel knowledge. 

Index Terms —Molecular communication, neural sequence de-tection, constrained coding, run-length-limited codes, masked learning. 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

Molecular communication (MC) conveys data by emitting molecules that propagate from a transmitter to a receiver, and is being studied for nanoscale and in-body links where conventional electromagnetic methods are difficult to deploy [1]–[3]. In diffusion-based MC, the molecules emitted for one bit do not all arrive within that bit’s interval: some are captured at once, others arrive over later intervals, and a fraction is never captured. The late arrivals interfere with subsequent bits, so the count measured in any interval depends on several past bits, and reliable reception is a sequence-detection problem rather than a per-bit one. The simplest MC receivers threshold each received count, which ignores the channel memory that diffusion creates [3]. Detectors that exploit this memory optimally need accurate channel state information (CSI)—the diffusion coefficient, geometry, and noise level—which is hard to obtain on a small MC node [4]. The sliding bidirectional recurrent neural network (SBRNN) is well suited to it [5]: a bidirectional LSTM is run over a short window of received counts, producing for each in-window bit a probability mass function (PMF) over the transmitted bit, and as the window slides each bit is covered by many windows whose PMFs are averaged. Interference can also be reduced at the transmitter by constraining which bit patterns are sent [6], [7]. Run-length-limited ISI-mitigation (RLIM) coding does this [7]: from the run-length-limited words of a given length it keeps the 2k

of lowest Hamming weight, so the transmitted 1-bits are few and well separated, and under the standard MC power normalization each 1-bit can then be sent with more molecules. RLIM has been reported to outperform classical run-length-limited (RLL) and other prominent MC codes over a wide range of conditions [7], and a later enumerative construction made large block lengths practical on small devices [8]; we therefore use it as the sole coded baseline. Crucially, these RLIM gains, like those of MC channel codes generally, were measured against threshold detection. Since the SBRNN is among the strongest MC detectors, we re-examine the comparison with it: we evaluate uncoded and RLIM-coded transmission under the same SBRNN receiver, and find that the best RLIM-SBRNN receiver still beats the best uncoded one and improves substantially over threshold-detected RLIM. As an idealized detection method we also include a channel-state-aware maximum-likelihood sequence detector (MLSE). As the most important contribution of this paper, we in-troduce a training rule for the SBRNN that exploits the run-length structure of RLIM. In an order-i RLIM code, every 

1-bit is followed by i 0-bits. Because these positions are fixed by the code, the RLIM decoder overwrites them with 0-bits before decoding, regardless of what the detector outputs there, as long as the preceding 1-bit was detected correctly. They are also the hardest positions for the detector: lying just after an emission, their counts are inflated by the molecules of that 

1-bit and resemble a transmitted 1-bit. Training the network to label them therefore would waste the precious capacity on outputs the decoder already discards. Our proposed mask simply removes these forced-zero positions from the training loss, thereby allowing the compact neural detector to focus on the most important bit positions. Neural detection and decoding of constrained or coded sequences has been studied outside MC, e.g., for RLL-coded partial-response channels [9] and constrained-sequence codes [10], but neither removes code-determined positions from the detector’s training loss. Dropping the positions where mt is 0 is justified by the RLIM error correction rule of Section II: a detected 1-bit forces the next i positions to 0 before decoding, so, provided that 1-bit is itself correct, the detector’s outputs there are dis-carded and cannot affect the decoded word. Training on them is wasted effort on the typical, correctly-detected case. The argument needs the 1-bits to be detected reliably, which holds well in MC, because a 1-bit triggers an emission, so its first interval (assuming p1, as defined in (2), is the largest channel coefficient) has, on average, the largest corresponding count and thus is the easiest position to detect. This differs from the neural detector for RLL-coded partial-response channels [9] and the neural decoder for constrained-sequence codes [10], neither of which masks code-determined positions in training; our mask instead drops these positions from the detector’s loss and is specific to the RLIM code structure. We also include an idealized MLSE as a benchmark, not a deployable receiver: it must know the channel exactly— the diffusion coefficient, geometry, and noise—which a real MC receiver can hardly know. Optimal MLSE runs the Viterbi algorithm on a trellis whose state is the full channel memory [4], [16]. With I = 100 retained taps, this trellis is expo-nentially large, so we use a reduced-state detector [17] that keeps the 12 strongest (i.e., initial) taps in the state and treats the rest as noise. For its branch metric, the count from each interfering 1-bit is a binomial variable, the marginal of the multinomial emission (3), and the interval count is a sum of such independent binomials plus noise, which for the molecule counts here is approximated by a Gaussian. We use this Gaussian metric, although the exact sum-of-binomials metric could replace it at a substantially higher per-branch cost. In cost, the MLSE searches a 212 = 4096 -state trellis per bit and needs the channel taps, with a state count exponential in the modelled memory, whereas the compact SBRNN runs at a fixed 2498 -parameter cost independent of the channel memory and uses no CSI. The SBRNN is trained in two ways. For the channel sweeps in Figs. 2(a)–(d), one model per method and loss is trained on the pooled data of all 32 operating points ( 10 5 bits each), so it learns one cross-condition rule. The window, model-size, and training-size sweeps are trained per point, since their axes change the learning problem. The default detector is the 2498 -parameter model of Table II with window length L = 40 . This is large enough to span the dominant ISI taps and past the point where the window sweeps in Figs. 2(e)–(f) gain further. We train each SBRNN with Adam (learning rate 10 −3, batch size 500 ) for 20 epochs, by which the training loss had largely flattened in our runs. Counts are standardized before inference. Each point is tested on 4 × 10 6 bits, except r0 = 9 .5 μm in Fig. 2(c), which uses 2.5 × 10 8 bits for all methods and also except M = 600 in Fig. 2(a), which uses 5 × 10 7 bits for MLSE methods. Coding and detection help in complementary ways: RLIM spaces the emissions and adds error correction, the SBRNN reads the count memory a threshold method discards, and the mask focuses the SBRNN training on the bits that carry important information. In the distance sweep, Fig. 2(c), the point r0 = 9 .5 μm drives several RLIM-SBRNN variants into the ≈ 10 −7 range. The model-size and training-size sweeps, Figs. 2(g)–(h), show that the 2498 -parameter default is already strong and that performance mostly settles once 

10 5 training bits are used. Fig. 2(g) also shows that the performance gap between masked and unmasked SBRNN for RLIM-coded transmissions mostly shrinks as the network complexity increases. This further justifies the use of our proposed masking in the tested compact SBRNN regime, suitable for resource-constrained nano-scale environments. Fig. 2 also includes the MLSE detection. V. C ONCLUSION 

This letter shows that a constrained code can do more than correct detector errors: it can also reshape the learning problem faced by a neural receiver. By removing RLIM’s deterministically forced-zero positions from the loss, a com-pact 2498 -parameter SBRNN allocates its limited capacity to positions where the transmitted bits remain informative. The numerical gains are substantial: without channel knowledge, the resulting detector can match or outperform channel-state-aware MLSE on coded sequences; the proposed masking improves the unmasked SBRNN in 227 of 236 comparisons; and the best RLIM-SBRNN beats the best uncoded receiver in 46 of 59 operating points, with the best observed RLIM-SBRNN result reaching the ≈ 10 −7 BER range under an ISI-limited setting. More broadly, these results suggest that decoder-implied constraints can provide useful training infor-mation for compact neural receivers in resource-constrained molecular and nanoscale communication.
