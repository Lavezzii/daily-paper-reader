# Towards Intelligent Wireless Networks: The Synergy of Generative AI and Digital Twins 

Afan Ali, Ali Arshad Nasir, Senior Member, IEEE , Naveed Iqbal, Senior Member, IEEE ,and Daniel Benevides da Costa, Senior Member, IEEE 

Abstract —This paper proposes a generative AI (GenAI)-enabled digital twin (DT) framework for proactive and energy-aware wireless optimization in future 6G ecosystems. Most ex-isting AI-assisted DT approaches remain fundamentally reactive, adjusting network parameters only after performance degrada-tion occurs or restricting GenAI to isolated signal-level tasks such as channel estimation. This work adopts a proactive approach. Instead of responding to problems after they appear, the proposed framework continuously synchronizes channel states, mobility dynamics, traffic conditions, and energy information within a real-time DT environment, enabling the system to anticipate con-gestion, interference, and energy demand before they materialize. The result is a closed-loop proactive architecture that operates at the system level, jointly managing communication, mobility, and resource dynamics for autonomous wireless control. Evaluations on a UAV-assisted non-terrestrial network (NTN) scenario show approximately 69.2% energy savings over reactive baselines while maintaining reliable quality-of-service (QoS) under dense and mobility-intensive conditions. Beyond this specific scenario, the framework offers a scalable foundation for broader AI-native 6G applications, including aerial platforms, autonomous systems, extended reality (XR), industrial automation, and space-air-ground-sea (SAGS) integrated infrastructures. 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

# THE transition toward sixth-generation (6G) wireless sys-tems is reshaping the role of communication networks. Sensing, localization, computing, and intelligent decision-making are expected to operate within a unified framework, serving applications ranging from extended reality (XR) and industrial automation to autonomous systems and space-air-ground-sea (SAGS) integrated networks [1]. Meeting these demands requires a fundamental rethinking of how networks are managed and optimized. Digital twin (DT) technology and generative artificial intel-ligence (GenAI) have emerged as two of the most promising enablers for this transition [2]. Industry has already rec-ognized this potential, with NVIDIA introducing AI-native wireless DTs through its Omniverse and Aerial platforms for large-scale network emulation [3], and Keysight Tech-nologies demonstrating radio frequency (RF) DTs combining ray tracing and hardware-in-the-loop evaluation for massive multiple-input multiple-output (MIMO) and 6G research [4]. Rather than operating from instantaneous observations alone, the DT maintains an updated view of the system and supports predictive decision-making [2]. This distinguishes it from traditional simulation frameworks, which are typically static and disconnected from live network operation. The growing complexity of future wireless systems, in-cluding massive MIMO, reconfigurable intelligent surface (RIS)-assisted communications, UAV-enabled networking, and NTNs, has accelerated the adoption of DT technologies. In these environments, DTs enable efficient modeling of wireless channels, mobility patterns, network resources, and environmental interactions, supporting predictive optimization and autonomous network orchestration under highly dynamic conditions. A wireless DT operates across three interconnected layers that jointly define how the wireless environment is represented and updated. 

1) Physical Scene Layer: The physical scene layer forms the geometric foundation of the DT. It captures buildings, ob-stacles, roads, terrain, and user locations. High-fidelity three-dimensional modeling tools, geographic information systems (GIS), and real-time rendering engines are now widely avail-able for this purpose. NVIDIA Omniverse enables physics-aware environment generation for wireless simulation, while ray-tracing tools such as Sionna RT and Remcom Wire-less InSite provide geometry-aware propagation modeling for large-scale wireless environments. Real-world data sources including OpenStreetMap (OSM), LiDAR scans, and satellite imagery are increasingly used to construct realistic wireless scenes. This layer is relatively mature compared to other DT components. 

2) Propagation Layer: The propagation layer models how electromagnetic waves interact with the wireless environment, covering path loss, blockage, multipath propagation, interfer-ence evolution, and channel dynamics. This layer becomes particularly challenging in UAV-assisted and non-terrestrial deployments, where communication geometry changes contin-uously as users move and aerial platforms reposition. Tradi-tional planar-wave assumptions and stochastic fading models are increasingly difficult to maintain under such conditions. Recent work on map-based channel models and channel knowledge maps (CKMs) has explored spatial information for channel prediction and CSI reconstruction [14]. Physics-consistent ray tracing has gained traction for reproducing real-istic propagation behavior in complex dynamic environments. 

3) Synchronization and Twin-State Layer: The synchro-nization layer is both the most critical and the least developed component of the DT architecture. It continuously updates the DT using real-time observations from the physical network, integrating CSI, mobility information, traffic conditions, and environmental measurements into a unified twin-state repre-sentation. Ericsson has explored AI-driven network manage-ment for autonomous operation, and Keysight has demon-strated RF DT platforms capable of integrating ray tracing and predictive analytics [4]. At the standardization level, 3GPP has initiated discussions on AI-native architectures, NDTs, and DT-assisted operation for future 6G systems. In practice, this layer determines whether the DT functions as a passive monitoring system or as an active intelligence platform capable of proactive optimization.

GenAI has gained significant attention as a transforma-tive paradigm for intelligent wireless communications. Un-like traditional AI models focused on classification or re-gression, GenAI models generate new data representations, learn complex distributions, and synthesize realistic wireless environments [9], [11]. Large language models (LLMs), such as, OpenAI ChatGPT and Google Gemini demonstrate how generative models can learn broad contextual relationships from large-scale datasets. C. From Signal-Level to System-Level: The Missing Link 

System-level dynamics are often treated as secondary con-cerns in existing GenAI-based wireless research. Many ap-proaches assume static environments, quasi-static channels, or isolated communication links. Real wireless systems behave very differently. User mobility evolves continuously, traffic demand fluctuates, and congestion appears and disappears dynamically. These effects are especially pronounced in UAV-assisted and non-terrestrial deployments operating under lim-ited energy budgets, where communication quality, energy consumption, and network lifetime are tightly coupled. Several important challenges therefore remain insufficiently addressed. Existing GenAI frameworks rarely tackle long-term network evolution, predictive resource management, or proac-tive wireless control at the system level. The problem is no longer limited to accurately reconstructing the current channel. The network must also anticipate how conditions will evolve and adapt before performance degradation or excessive energy consumption occurs. This observation directly motivates the framework proposed in this work. 

D. Emerging Role of GenAI in Wireless Digital Twins 

A new research direction is beginning to take shape. Rather than using GenAI solely for signal reconstruction, recent studies have started exploring how generative models can support system-level intelligence through DTs and network automation [7], [8], [13]. In this context, wireless NDTs are increasingly viewed as synchronized virtual replicas capable of supporting network monitoring, predictive analysis, and adap-tive optimization. GenAI thus evolves from a reconstruction engine into a predictive intelligence layer capable of learning network behavior and anticipating future operating conditions. As illustrated in Fig. 1, this evolution highlights the central role of GenAI-enabled DTs in future wireless systems, where generative foundation models act as a core intelligence engine that seamlessly bridges the physical and virtual domains, enabling closed-loop optimization, proactive decision-making, and a wide range of intelligent applications. This trend is visible in both industrial and standardization activities. Current 3GPP discussions on NDTs, integrated sensing and communication (ISAC), and AI-native wireless operation suggest that future 6G systems will rely increasingly on network-level intelligence rather than isolated physical-layer optimization. Similar ideas appear in DT-assisted smart city frameworks and AI-driven resource orchestration for heterogeneous wireless environments. However, a meaningful gap remains. Dynamic and closed-loop system intelligence is comparatively underexplored, particularly in UAV-assisted and non-terrestrial scenarios where mobility, traffic evolution, aerial geometry, and limited onboard energy continuously shape network behavior. 

1) cGAN as a Predictive Control Engine: Among the available generative models, cGANs offer an attractive balance between modeling capability and computational efficiency for real-time wireless control. While cGANs have been explored for channel estimation and radio map generation [5], the proposed framework assigns cGAN a fundamentally different role. III. P ROPOSED GEN AI-E NABLED DIGITAL TWIN 

FRAMEWORK 

The preceding section established that existing approaches either operate reactively at the system level or confine GenAI to signal-level tasks. The framework proposed in this paper is designed specifically to overcome both limitations. Rather than treating DT synchronization and GenAI inference as separate processes, it integrates them into a single closed-loop proactive control system, as illustrated in Fig. 2. The design operates at the system level, jointly managing physical wireless environments, real-time DT synchronization, predic-tive GenAI modeling, and adaptive resource control within a unified architecture. 

A. DT-Assisted Dataset Generation for GenAI Pretraining 

Training a reliable GenAI engine for wireless network control requires large-scale datasets that capture a wide range of operating conditions, including rare congestion events, rapid mobility transitions, and extreme interference scenarios. Collecting such data from live deployments alone is imprac-tical due to measurement cost, deployment complexity, and the inherent difficulty of observing rare network events in controlled settings. The DT layer addresses this challenge directly by acting as a scalable wireless data generation platform. Based on continuously synchronized DT states, the framework gener-ates large-scale synthetic datasets covering both normal and extreme operating conditions. The DT can emulate varying user densities, mobility patterns, blockage conditions, traffic fluctuations, propagation environments, and energy utilization profiles that would be difficult or impossible to capture through real-world measurement campaigns alone. Combining real-world observations with DT-assisted syn-thetic generation offers four concrete advantages for the proposed framework. It enables scalable dataset construction for AI-native wireless systems. It improves the diversity of training conditions seen by the GenAI engine. It enhances model generalization by exposing the cGAN to a broader range of wireless environment dynamics. Most importantly, it allows the GenAI engine to learn predictive system-level behavior suitable for proactive and energy-aware network optimization in future 6G deployments. 

B. Offline cGAN Training and Online Deployment 

With the dataset established through DT-assisted genera-tion, the cGAN is trained offline using multimodal wireless observations. The training data includes CSI measurements, mobility traces, traffic evolution, propagation conditions, in-terference states, and energy utilization information. During JOURNAL OF L ATEX CLASS FILES, VOL. , NO. , MAY 2026 5

training, the generator learns a conditional mapping between synchronized DT states and target system outputs such as congestion evolution, energy demand, interference variation, and adaptive resource allocation decisions. The discriminator runs in parallel, ensuring that generated predictions remain statistically consistent with realistic wireless system behavior. Offline training serves two important purposes. First, it allows the framework to leverage DT-generated data covering rare and hard-to-observe operating conditions without requir-ing live network exposure during training. Second, it improves model generalization by exposing the cGAN to a diverse range of wireless environments and deployment scenarios before online deployment begins. Once training is complete, the cGAN is deployed online within the closed-loop DT architecture. At this stage, the model performs real-time inference by conditioning on the cur-rent twin-state representation and producing forward-looking predictions of network evolution. This separation of offline training and online inference is a deliberate design choice. It keeps inference latency low, which is essential for real-time proactive wireless control, while still benefiting from the rich generalization learned during the offline training phase. 

C. Closed-Loop Operation and Proactive Control Mechanism 

The full closed-loop operation of the proposed framework is illustrated in Fig. 2. The physical wireless environment contin-uously streams real-time observations to the DT layer, which updates the twin-state representation accordingly. The cGAN then conditions on this updated twin state to predict how the network will evolve over the near future, including anticipated congestion, interference buildup, and energy demand. Based on these predictions, proactive control decisions are generated and applied back to the physical network, completing the optimization loop. This closed-loop design addresses a funda-mental weakness of conventional reactive strategies. Reactive approaches adapt only after degradation has already occurred, meaning the network is perpetually responding to problems rather than preventing them. The proposed framework inverts this relationship. By inferring future network states before they materialize, the system can reduce transmission power during predicted low-demand periods, preemptively reallocate resources ahead of anticipated congestion, and extend battery lifetime in energy-constrained deployments such as UAV-assisted NTN systems. The framework is designed to be application agnostic. The same closed-loop architecture applies across smart cam-puses, industrial Internet of Things (IoT) infrastructures, ISAC networks, immersive XR platforms, UAV-supported links, autonomous systems, and SAGS-enabled architectures. This generality is a direct consequence of the system-level design philosophy, which operates on synchronized network state rep-resentations rather than application-specific signal structures. C ASE STUDY : P ROACTIVE GEN AI-E NABLED DT FRAMEWORK FOR UAV B ATTERY LIFETIME 

OPTIMIZATION IN NTN S YSTEMS 

Having described the architecture and operational principles of the proposed proactive GenAI-enabled DT framework, we now validate its performance through a focused case study. Among the many potential application scenarios identified in Section III-C, UAV battery lifetime optimization in NTN systems represents a particularly stringent test case, as it simultaneously stresses the framework’s DT synchronization accuracy, predictive horizon fidelity, and energy-aware control responsiveness. 

A. Simulation Setup and System Configuration 

We consider a 3GPP Rel-18/19 NTN deployment operating at the Ka-band carrier frequency of 26 GHz, consistent with TR 38.811 and TR 38.821 design assumptions. To keep the considered scenario closer to practical NTN and low-altitude UAV deployments, we configure the UAV-mounted base sta-tion with an 8 × 8 uniform planar array (UPA) and constrain the UAV to operate at a nominal altitude of 100 m, which aligns with realistic low-altitude aerial communication settings discussed in recent 3GPP NTN studies. The UAV follows a trajectory designed to provide balanced coverage over a 

400 m service area while avoiding excessive hovering-related energy consumption. Ground users follow a clustered random waypoint (RWP) mobility process with pedestrian speed of 1

m/s according to the 3GPP TS 38.901 mobility assumption. Figs. 3a and 3b illustrate 3D and top-view of the UAV-BS NTN scenario respectively. To further emulate realistic NTN traffic behavior, the number of active users varies dynamically over time, resulting in heterogeneous propagation conditions, mobility patterns, and traffic load fluctuations. Minimum mean square error (MMSE)-based channel estimation is employed for channel reconstruction, while Kalman filtering is used for mobility prediction and state tracking. The generator adopts a residual-block architecture conditioned on UAV position, mobility features, estimated channel amplitude, traffic load, and battery state-of-charge (SoC). To improve adversarial training stability, the discriminator follows a Wasserstein GAN with Gradient Penalty (WGAN-GP) design. Based on the learned DT representations, the cGAN predicts future channel quality several steps ahead of the physical system. This predictive capability fundamentally differentiates the proposed framework from conventional reactive wireless optimization approaches. When excess SINR margin is predicted, the con-troller proactively reduces transmit power to conserve battery energy. In contrast, when channel degradation is anticipated, the framework restores transmission power before quality-of-service (QoS) violations occur.

C. Computational Complexity and Scalability Considerations 

While the proposed framework delivers substantial energy and QoS gains, it is important to acknowledge that real-time DT synchronization and cGAN inference introduce non-trivial computational overhead. Continuously ingesting and updating CSI, mobility, traffic, and energy states places demands on both backhaul capacity and edge computing resources, and these costs grow with network density and user mobility. Notably, this overhead is precisely what enables the perfor-mance advantage over simpler baselines. Reactive and partial schemes, such as, DT-only or cGAN-only approaches avoid this complexity but pay a steep price in adaptation accuracy, as they lack either the predictive capability or the real-time environmental grounding needed for proactive control. The offline training strategy adopted here deliberately offloads the heaviest computation away from the live control loop, keeping online inference latency low enough for real-time operation while still benefiting from rich generalization learned during training. The approximately 69% energy saving over the reactive baseline suggests considerable headroom before such overhead would erode the net benefit, yet scaling to larger multi-UAV or heterogeneous deployments will require further work on model compression and hardware-aware co-design. V. C ONCLUSIONS AND FUTURE RESEARCH DIRECTIONS 

This paper proposed a proactive wireless system framework built upon the synergy between GenAI and DTs. By com-bining the real-time environmental awareness of DTs with the predictive learning capability of GenAI, the proposed framework enabled proactive and energy-efficient system-level wireless optimization under highly dynamic operating condi-tions. The framework was sufficiently modular and scalable for broader wireless scenarios, including XR services, industrial automation, autonomous systems, smart campuses, and SAGS-integrated networks, where energy-efficient and adaptive net-work operation had become increasingly important. Future research directions include foundation-model-driven wireless intelligence, cooperative multi-agent DT ecosystems with dis-tributed UAV coordination, AI-native ISAC, semantic-aware networking, and self-evolving autonomous 6G infrastructures capable of predictive reasoning, cross-domain orchestration, and real-time environmental adaptation.
